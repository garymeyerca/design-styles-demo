The Catechism

A 17th-century aesthetic of grandeur and drama — gilded scrollwork, swirling acanthus, deep chiaroscuro shadow and rich crimson. It rejects restraint entirely: every surface is an opportunity for ornament and theatre.

With discipline, yes. This page keeps a strict hierarchy: gold leads the eye, crimson signals action, and parchment text stays legible. The maximalism is staged, not chaotic — and focus rings keep every control operable.

Showcases, luxury brands, cultural and music products, and any moment that wants to feel ceremonial. It is the architecture of a palace — best where occasion and grandeur are the point.

The Doctrine

Three tenets of theatrical excess

Baroque is grandeur with intent — drama composed, not scattered.

I

Gild without mercy

Acanthus scrolls, curling frames and leaf-gold filigree crown every edge. Ornament is not decoration laid on top — it is the structure.

II

Stage the shadow

Chiaroscuro rules the page: deep chocolate dark, a single dramatic light, and gold that glows as if struck by a candle.

III

Compose the excess

Maximalism only works with hierarchy. Gold leads, crimson commands, parchment speaks — every flourish placed for effect.
