FAQ · 013

Questions on the system

Geometric design uses pure shapes — circles, triangles, arcs, lines — as structural devices and not just as decoration. The composition reads as edited, the grid stays visible, and every element earns its place.

It descends from both — but loosens the primary-colour discipline of Bauhaus and the type-first rigour of Swiss editorial. Closer to contemporary brand work: warmer paper, a single bright accent, asymmetric compositions, more breathing room.

It rewards confident editorial content — long-form, dashboards, product storytelling. It breaks the moment you crowd the grid: too many shapes and the composition becomes pattern, not statement.

Section 03 — Principles

Three rules. Held strictly.

Geometric design only works when the discipline holds. Drop one rule and the page slips back into pattern.

Read time02m
Rule 01

Primitives only.

Every element reduces to a circle, a triangle, a square, or a line. No bevels. No drop shadows. No texture. The shape carries the meaning.

Rule 02

The grid is visible.

Composition rides on a strict modular grid — 12 columns, an 8-pixel baseline. Asymmetry is allowed, but every edge lines up with something.

Rule 03

One accent, used sparingly.

Two dominant neutrals — paper and ink — and a single bright accent. The accent appears on roughly five percent of the page. Never more.
