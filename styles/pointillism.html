FAQ

A painting technique pioneered by Georges Seurat and Paul Signac in the 1880s: images are built from countless tiny dots of pure, unmixed colour placed side by side, which the eye blends optically into luminous form.

Optical mixing keeps every colour vivid — a green from blue and yellow dots shimmers in a way a pre-mixed green never can. Surfaces feel alive and atmospheric rather than printed and inert.

Calm, contemplative interfaces — galleries, reading rooms, media players — where a soft, luminous, painterly texture rewards a slower, closer look.

Three rules, one shimmer

Pointillism is patience — luminosity built dot by dot.

Rule 01

Pure, unmixed colour

Pigments are never blended on the palette. Each dot is a single pure hue — mixing happens only in the viewer's eye.

Rule 02

Optical blending

Step back and the dots fuse into form and light. Step close and the surface dissolves into a constellation of colour.

Rule 03

Luminous patience

Every surface is built from radial-gradient stipple — soft, atmospheric, and unmistakably hand-placed.
